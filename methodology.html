Heads up: The formulas below are a deliberate simplification of reality. Tax rates, mortgage amortisation, and market growth are modelled as deterministic yearly steps. Use this as a planning lens, not a financial recommendation.
Methodology

How Helmworth projects your net worth

Every number you see in the planner — current net worth, the projection chart, the stats table, your Coast FIRE and FIRE ages — is produced by a small, pure calculation engine. This page documents exactly what that engine does, in math.

  • Time is discrete and yearly. Year \(0\) is today; year \(y\) is the end of year \(y\).
  • All rates are decimals in the engine: \(0.07\) means 7 %.
  • Every formula is pure: same inputs always produce the same outputs.
  • Default horizon age: \(100\). Default retirement age: \(67\). Safe withdrawal rate: \(0.04\).

1. Notation & conventions

SymbolMeaning
\(a\)An asset (one of eight classes; see §2).
\(A\)The full set of assets currently held.
\(y\)Year index, \(y \in \{0, 1, \ldots, H - a_0\}\).
\(a_0\)The user's starting age, userInfo.age.
\(H\)Horizon age (default \(100\)).
\(R\)Retirement age (default \(67\)).
\(m_0\)Today's monthly expenses, userInfo.monthlyExpenses.
\(\iota\)Inflation rate (decimal), userInfo.inflationRate.
\(\ell\)A single lot, \(\ell = (\text{value}, \text{costBasis}, \text{year})\).
\(\Pi_y\)Total passive income produced in year \(y\).
\(E_y\)Total expenses in year \(y\) (includes residence costs).
\(D_y\)Total personal-debt payments made during year \(y\).
\(W_y\)Net worth at the end of year \(y\).

Throughout this page, \(x'\) denotes the value of \(x\) after the yearly step. The expression \((1+r)^n\) is computed with Math.pow. Capital losses never produce a tax credit: gains are clamped at \(0\) before applying CGT.

2. Data model

The engine consumes the following state:

state = {
  userInfo: {
    age:             integer,
    retirementAge:   integer,    // default 67
    country:         string,
    monthlyExpenses: number ≥ 0,
    inflationRate:   decimal,    // e.g. 0.02
  },
  assets: Asset[],
}

There are eight asset classes. Their net-worth contribution is:

Class Liquid? Lots? Net-worth contribution \(\nu(a)\)
stocks yesyes\(\sum_{\ell \in a} \ell.\text{value}\)
bonds yesyes\(\sum_{\ell \in a} \ell.\text{value}\)
crypto yesyes\(\sum_{\ell \in a} \ell.\text{value}\)
cash yesno \(a.\text{value}\)
realEstate no no \(a.\text{value} - a.\text{mortgageBalance}\)
privateBusinessnono\(a.\text{value}\)
pension n/ano \(0\) (income-only)
personalDebtn/ano\(-\,a.\text{balance}\)

A lot is a single purchase: \(\ell = (\text{value},\ \text{costBasis},\ \text{year})\). Adding a contribution appends a new lot whose cost basis equals the contributed amount. Lots are required for the HIFO drawdown described in §9.

3. Net worth

Net worth is the sum of per-asset contributions:

\[ W(A) \;=\; \sum_{a \in A} \nu(a) \]

Equivalently, expanded by class:

\[ W \;=\; \big(S + B + C + \$ + \mathrm{RE}_{\text{val}} + \mathrm{PB}_{\text{val}}\big) \;-\; \big(\mathrm{Debt} + \mathrm{Mortgage}\big) \]

where \(S, B, C\) are the lot-summed values of stocks, bonds, and crypto; \(\$\) is cash; \(\mathrm{RE}_{\text{val}}\) and \(\mathrm{PB}_{\text{val}}\) are the gross values of real estate and private business; \(\mathrm{Mortgage}\) is the sum of outstanding mortgage balances; and \(\mathrm{Debt}\) is the sum of personal-debt balances.

4. Inflation

Monthly expenses grow geometrically:

\[ m_y \;=\; m_0 \cdot (1 + \iota)^{\,y} \]

Asset values are not inflated by \(\iota\). Each asset class has its own growth rate (appreciation, dividend growth, pension revaluation) applied inside its step function. This avoids double-counting.

5. Yearly time-stepping (Standard scenario)

The simulator returns a trajectory of length \(H - a_0 + 1\). Index \(0\) is the starting snapshot (no growth applied); index \(y \ge 1\) is the end of year \(y\).

5.1 Year \(0\) — snapshot

\[ W_0 = \sum_{a \in A} \nu(a), \qquad \Pi_0 = 0, \qquad E_0 = 12 \cdot m_0, \qquad D_0 = 0 \]

5.2 Year \(y \in \{1, \ldots, H - a_0\}\)

Define the per-year context \(\mathrm{ctx} = (y,\ a_0 + y,\ \mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}})\) — the year index, the user's current age, and a contribution flag. For the Standard scenario, \(\mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}}\) is true while \(a_0 + y\) is below the retirement cutoff and false afterward.

For each asset \(a\), call its class-specific step function (§6):

\[ (a',\ \pi_a,\ \kappa_a,\ \delta_a) \;=\; \mathrm{step}_{\mathrm{class}(a)}(a,\ \mathrm{ctx}) \]

where \(\pi_a\) is the asset's passive income, \(\kappa_a\) is an extra expense (only set by residence real-estate; \(0\) otherwise), and \(\delta_a\) is the asset's debt payment for the year (only set by personalDebt; \(0\) otherwise).

After stepping, sales scheduled for year \(y\) fire (§8). Then aggregate:

\[ \begin{aligned} \Pi_y &= \sum_{a} \pi_a, &\qquad D_y &= \sum_{a:\ \mathrm{class}(a)=\text{personalDebt}} \delta_a, \\ K_y &= \sum_{a:\ \text{residence}} \kappa_a, &\qquad E_y &= 12 \cdot m_0 \cdot (1 + \iota)^{y} \;+\; K_y, \\ W_y &= W(A') \end{aligned} \]

The Standard scenario stops contributions automatically once \(a_0 + y \ge \min\!\big(\,\text{retirementAge},\ \min\{\text{pension.startingAge}\},\ 67\big)\). Coast FIRE (§10) and FIRE (§11) override this rule.

6. Per-asset-class step functions

Each \(\mathrm{step}_X(a, \mathrm{ctx})\) is pure and returns \((a',\ \pi)\), plus optional \(\kappa\) (residence) or \(\delta\) (personal debt). Below, all variables refer to fields of \(a\).

6.1 Stocks

Parameters: average return \(r\), yearly contribution \(c\).

For every existing lot \(\ell\):

\[ \ell.\text{value}' = \ell.\text{value} \cdot (1 + r), \qquad \ell.\text{costBasis}' = \ell.\text{costBasis}, \qquad \ell.\text{year}' = \ell.\text{year} \]

If \(\mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}} \wedge c > 0\), append a new lot:

\[ \ell_{\mathrm{new}} = (c,\ c,\ y), \qquad \pi = 0 \]

Example. 10 years of 7 % growth on \(10\,000\) with no contribution: \(10\,000 \cdot 1.07^{10} \approx 19\,671.51\).

6.2 Bonds

Parameters: yield \(\rho\), yield-tax rate \(\tau_\rho\), yearly contribution \(c\).

Yield is computed on the value before the new contribution lot is added:

\[ V \;=\; \sum_{\ell} \ell.\text{value}, \qquad \pi \;=\; V \cdot \rho \cdot (1 - \tau_\rho) \]

Existing lots are kept flat (no appreciation in v1):

\[ \ell.\text{value}' = \ell.\text{value}, \qquad \ell.\text{costBasis}' = \ell.\text{costBasis} \]

If \(\mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}} \wedge c > 0\), append \(\ell_{\mathrm{new}} = (c, c, y)\).

Example. \(100\,000\) at 4 % yield with 20 % yield tax: \(100\,000 \cdot 0.04 \cdot 0.80 = 3\,200\).

6.3 Crypto

Mathematically identical to Stocks: lots grow by \((1 + r)\), optional contribution lot, and \(\pi = 0\). The class is kept separate to allow future extensions (staking, on-chain yield, etc.).

6.4 Cash

\[ \text{value}' = \text{value}, \qquad \pi = 0 \]

Cash earns no interest in v1 — a deliberately conservative choice that makes the opportunity cost of holding cash visible in the projection.

6.5 Real estate

Parameters: appreciation \(\alpha\), mortgage balance \(M\), repayment fraction \(\rho_M\), cash flow \(\mathrm{CF}\), yearly costs \(K\), kind \(\kappa_p \in \{\text{investment},\ \text{residence}\}\).

\[ \text{value}' = \text{value} \cdot (1 + \alpha), \qquad M' = \max\!\big(0,\ M \cdot (1 - \rho_M)\big) \]

Income / expense splits by kind:

\[ \pi \;=\; \begin{cases} \max(\mathrm{CF},\ 0) & \text{if } \kappa_p = \text{investment} \\ 0 & \text{if } \kappa_p = \text{residence} \end{cases} \qquad \kappa_a \;=\; \begin{cases} \max(-\mathrm{CF},\ 0) & \text{if } \kappa_p = \text{investment} \\ K & \text{if } \kappa_p = \text{residence} \end{cases} \]

For an investment property, a positive cash flow is reported as passive income and a negative one as an extra expense (never as negative passive income). The two cases never both fire, so \(\pi\) and \(\kappa_a\) cannot both be positive in the same year. The mortgage has no interest accrual in this model — a fixed fraction \(\rho_M\) of the outstanding balance is repaid each year. Any interest the user actually pays is expected to already be netted into \(\mathrm{CF}\).

6.6 Private business

Parameters: value growth \(g\), yearly dividend \(D\), dividend growth \(g_D\), dividend-tax rate \(\tau_D\).

The current year's dividend is paid before growth is applied to \(D\):

\[ \pi = D \cdot (1 - \tau_D), \qquad \text{value}' = \text{value} \cdot (1 + g), \qquad D' = D \cdot (1 + g_D) \]

6.7 Pension

Parameters: yearly amount \(P\), revaluation rate \(\rho_P\), starting age \(s\). Let \(\mathrm{age} = a_0 + y\) be the user's current age.

\[ \pi \;=\; \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \mathrm{age} < s \\[2pt] P \cdot (1 + \rho_P)^{\,\mathrm{age} - s} & \text{if } \mathrm{age} \ge s \end{cases} \qquad a' = a \]

Pension contributes nothing to net worth (it is an income flow with no balance to sell). The amount is treated as net of tax: users should set \(P\) to their take-home figure.

6.8 Personal debt

Parameters: balance \(b\), annual interest \(r\), monthly payment \(P\), monthly rate \(r_m = r / 12\).

The balance is updated monthly (12 sub-steps per simulated year). For each \(m \in \{0, \ldots, 11\}\) while \(b > 0\):

\[ b_{\mathrm{grown}} = b \cdot (1 + r_m) \] \[ \mathrm{pay}_m = \begin{cases} b_{\mathrm{grown}} & \text{if } b_{\mathrm{grown}} \le P \quad \text{(final partial payment)} \\ P & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \] \[ b_{m+1} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } b_{\mathrm{grown}} \le P \\ b_{\mathrm{grown}} - P & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Yearly aggregates:

\[ \delta_a = \sum_{m=0}^{11} \mathrm{pay}_m, \qquad \pi = 0 \]

\(\delta_a\) is reported separately — it is a drag on cash flow, not passive income. Once the balance reaches \(0\), no further payments accrue.

Example. \(b = 10\,000\), \(r = 12\,\%\) (so \(r_m = 1\,\%\)), \(P = 500\): after 12 monthly steps, \(b \approx 4\,734.26\).

7. Passive income aggregation

For a given year \(y\) and asset set \(A\):

\[ \Pi^{(c)}_y \;=\; \sum_{a:\ \mathrm{class}(a)=c} \pi_a, \qquad \Pi_y \;=\; \sum_{c} \Pi^{(c)}_y \]

This breakdown drives the per-class series in the chart and stats table. The total \(\Pi_y\) is what the engine compares against expenses when deciding whether drawdown is required.

8. Sale events

Real-estate and private-business assets may carry a planned sale year. When saleYearsFromNow === y, the sale fires after the per-class step (and, in FIRE, before drawdown — so newly converted proceeds are immediately drawdown-eligible).

8.1 Sale proceeds

Let \(f\) be the sale fee fraction, \(\zeta\) the sale capital-gains-tax rate, and \(V_0\) the original purchase value (captured at asset creation):

\[ \mathrm{fees} = f \cdot \text{value}, \qquad \mathrm{gain} = \max(0,\ \text{value} - V_0), \qquad \mathrm{tax} = \zeta \cdot \mathrm{gain} \] \[ \mathrm{proceeds} = \begin{cases} \text{value} - \mathrm{fees} - \mathrm{tax} - M & \text{if class} = \text{realEstate} \\ \text{value} - \mathrm{fees} - \mathrm{tax} & \text{if class} = \text{privateBusiness} \end{cases} \]

Capital losses (value < V0) do not generate a tax credit; the \(\max\) clamps the gain at \(0\).

8.2 Conversion

The source asset's saleConversion field decides where the proceeds go. In all cases the source asset is removed.

  • Target asset — append \((\text{proceeds},\ \text{proceeds},\ y)\) as a new lot on the target (which must be lot-bearing: stocks, bonds, or crypto).
  • Inline parameters — create a brand-new asset of the requested class, seeded with the proceeds.
  • Unset — proceeds become a new cash asset named "<source.name> proceeds". No value is ever lost.

9. Drawdown & HIFO sale algorithm

In the FIRE scenario, when the year's expenses plus debt payments exceed passive income, the engine sells assets to cover the gap. Let \(S\) be the shortfall:

\[ S \;=\; E_y + D_y - \Pi_y \quad (S > 0 \text{ triggers drawdown}) \]

9.1 Drawdown order

  1. Drain cash first, in list order: \(\quad \mathrm{take}_a = \min(a.\text{value},\ S - \mathrm{drawn}), \quad a.\text{value}' = a.\text{value} - \mathrm{take}_a.\) Cash earns no return and pays no tax, so it has the lowest opportunity cost.
  2. If still short, define liquid-investable totals \(V_{\mathrm{liq}} = V_S + V_B + V_C\) where \(V_c = \sum_{a:\ \mathrm{class}(a)=c} \sum_{\ell \in a} \ell.\text{value}\). Allocate the remaining shortfall \(S_{\mathrm{rem}}\) proportionally: \[ T_c = S_{\mathrm{rem}} \cdot \frac{V_c}{V_{\mathrm{liq}}}. \]
  3. Within each class, distribute \(T_c\) across that class's assets in proportion to their values: \[ T_a = T_c \cdot \frac{V_a}{\sum_{a' \in c} V_{a'}}. \]
  4. For each asset, run HIFO sale (§9.2) to net \(T_a\) cash.

The drawdown is successful iff:

\[ S - \mathrm{drawn} \;\le\; \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon = 10^{-6} \]

If unsuccessful, the FIRE simulator records failedAtAge = currentAge and stops.

9.2 HIFO sale within one asset

Lots are processed in HIFO order: highest cost basis first; ties broken by ascending year for determinism. For each lot \(\ell\), while the cumulative net proceeds are below the target by more than \(10^{-9}\):

The per-unit gain ratio (gain dollars per dollar of value sold from this lot):

\[ \gamma \;=\; \max\!\left(0,\ \frac{\ell.\text{value} - \ell.\text{costBasis}}{\ell.\text{value}}\right) \]

The effective tax rate on each unit of value sold, and the corresponding net ratio:

\[ \tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; \gamma \cdot \mathrm{cgt}, \qquad \eta \;=\; 1 - \tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \]

If \(\eta \le 0\), the lot is skipped (a pathological case where taxes wipe out proceeds).

Gross-up: how much gross value to sell from this lot so the net equals what we still need:

\[ v \;=\; \min\!\left(\ell.\text{value},\ \frac{S_{\mathrm{rem}}}{\eta}\right) \]

Per-lot accounting:

\[ \mathrm{gainSold}_\ell = v \cdot \gamma, \qquad \mathrm{tax}_\ell = \mathrm{gainSold}_\ell \cdot \mathrm{cgt}, \qquad \mathrm{net}_\ell = v - \mathrm{tax}_\ell \]

If \(\lvert v - \ell.\text{value} \rvert < 10^{-9}\), the lot is fully consumed and dropped. Otherwise the surviving slice scales its cost basis proportionally:

\[ \ell.\text{value}' = \ell.\text{value} - v, \qquad \ell.\text{costBasis}' = \ell.\text{costBasis} \cdot \frac{\ell.\text{value} - v}{\ell.\text{value}} \]

Worked example. Two lots \(\big[(1000,\ 600),\ (1000,\ 900)\big]\); we need \(500\) net at \(\mathrm{cgt} = 0.20\). HIFO selects the \(\text{cb} = 900\) lot first. \(\gamma = 100/1000 = 0.10\), \(\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0.02\), \(\eta = 0.98\). \(v = 500/0.98 \approx 510.20\). Tax \(\approx 10.20\). Net \(\approx 500.00\). The lot with \(\text{cb} = 600\) is untouched.

10. Coast FIRE

Coast FIRE age is the earliest age \(X \ge a_0\) at which the user can stop contributing and still meet the 4 % rule at retirement age \(R\).

10.1 Piecewise simulation

\(\mathrm{simulateCoastFire}(s, X)\) is identical to the Standard simulation except for the contribution flag, which becomes:

\[ \mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}}(y) \;=\; \big(a_0 + y \le X\big) \]

10.2 Retirement age \(R\)

Determined by the first match in the following order:

\[ R \;=\; \begin{cases} \mathrm{opts.retirementAge} & \text{if explicitly provided} \\ \mathrm{userInfo.retirementAge} & \text{if set on the profile} \\ \min\{\,a.\mathrm{startingAge} : \mathrm{class}(a) = \text{pension}\,\} & \text{if any pension exists} \\ 67 & \text{otherwise (default)} \end{cases} \]

10.3 Feasibility test (4 % rule)

Run \(\mathrm{simulateCoastFire}(s, X)\) until age \(R\). Let \(W(R; X)\) and \(\Pi(R; X)\) be the resulting net worth and total passive income at retirement, and \(E(R)\) the inflated yearly expenses (including residence costs):

\[ E(R) \;=\; 12 \cdot m_0 \cdot (1 + \iota)^{\,R - a_0} \;+\; \sum_{a:\ \text{residence}} a.\mathrm{yearlyCosts} \]

The user is feasible at stop-age \(X\) iff:

\[ \boxed{\;\Pi(R; X) \;+\; 0.04 \cdot W(R; X) \;\ge\; E(R)\;} \]

10.4 Search

Linear scan over \(X \in [a_0,\ \min(R,\ H)]\); return the smallest feasible \(X\), or null if none exists. Monotonicity: if stopping at \(X\) works, stopping any later than \(X\) also works (more accumulation, same retirement target), so the search exits on the first true.

11. FIRE

FIRE asks a stronger question than Coast FIRE: not "can I coast to retirement?", but "can I stop earning now and live off my portfolio for the rest of the horizon?" This requires a year-by-year drawdown simulation rather than a single snapshot at \(R\).

11.1 \(\mathrm{simulateFire}(s,\ X_{\text{start}})\)

For \(y \in \{1, \ldots, H - a_0\}\):

  1. \(\mathrm{inDecum} = (a_0 + y \ge X_{\text{start}})\).
  2. Set \(\mathbb{1}_{\text{contrib}} = \neg\,\mathrm{inDecum}\) (contributions on before \(X_{\text{start}}\), off afterward).
  3. Step every asset; aggregate \(\Pi_y\), \(D_y\), \(K_y\).
  4. Apply year-\(y\) sales (§8) before drawdown, so converted proceeds are spendable.
  5. Compute expenses \(E_y = 12 \cdot m_0 \cdot (1+\iota)^y + K_y\).
  6. If inDecum, compute \(S_y = E_y + D_y - \Pi_y\). If \(S_y > 0\), call drawdownYear(A,\ S_y) (§9). If it fails, set failedAtAge = a0 + y and stop.

11.2 \(\mathrm{findFireAge}(s)\)

Linear scan from \(a_0\) to \(H\):

\[ \mathrm{findFireAge}(s) \;=\; \min\!\big\{\, X \in [a_0,\,H] \;\big|\; \mathrm{simulateFire}(s,\ X).\mathrm{failedAtAge} = \mathrm{null} \,\big\} \]

(or null if no \(X\) succeeds).

This relies on the monotonicity assumption: if FIRE succeeds at \(X\), it succeeds at \(X + k\) for all \(k \ge 0\). More accumulation and a shorter decumulation horizon can only help. The assumption holds for typical inputs.

12. Stats table

The stats table samples the Standard trajectory at fixed horizons \(\{h_1, h_2, \ldots\}\) (default \(\{0, 5, 10, 20\}\)). For horizon \(h_i\):

\[ \mathrm{rows}[c][i] \;=\; \mathrm{trajectory}[h_i].\mathrm{byClass}[c] \quad \text{(per non-pension class \(c\))} \] \[ \mathrm{rows.total}[i] \;=\; \mathrm{trajectory}[h_i].W \;=\; W_{h_i} \] \[ \mathrm{rows.yearlyExpenses}[i] \;=\; \mathrm{trajectory}[h_i].E \;=\; E_{h_i} \] \[ \mathrm{rows.monthlyExpenses}[i] \;=\; \frac{E_{h_i}}{12} \]

The pension row is special: since \(\nu(\text{pension}) = 0\) for every pension asset, a "net worth" entry would always be zero. Instead the table reports the pension's yearly income at each horizon, growing by \((1+\rho_P)\) each year after \(\mathrm{startingAge}\). The total row excludes pension by construction.
